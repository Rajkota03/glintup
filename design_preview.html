The Accidental Discovery of Penicillin
3 min read

In 1928, Scottish bacteriologist Alexander Fleming returned to his laboratory after a summer holiday to find something unexpected. A petri dish containing Staphylococcus bacteria had been contaminated by a mold — and the bacteria around the mold had been destroyed.

"One sometimes finds what one is not looking for."

The mold, later identified as Penicillium notatum, was producing a substance that killed bacteria. Fleming named it penicillin, though it would take another decade before Howard Florey and Ernst Boris Chain developed it into a usable medicine.

By 1945, penicillin was being mass-produced and saving countless lives during World War II. That same year, Fleming, Florey, and Chain shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for their work.

Today, penicillin and its derivatives remain among the most widely used antibiotics in the world — all because a scientist forgot to clean a petri dish before leaving on vacation.

— Smithsonian Magazine
